Assessment 08

90-day operational improvement roadmap

A practical sequence for improving control and resilience before introducing more automation.

Days 1–30: establish facts

Baseline incidents, change failures, alerts, recovery performance and recurring manual work.

  • Audit monitoring rules against incident history
  • Identify repeated incident signatures
  • Review failed changes by causal category
  • Map critical services and third parties

Days 31–60: strengthen controls

Remove structural weaknesses before adding technical complexity.

  • Embed suppression into planned-activity runbooks
  • Enforce rollback and non-production evidence
  • Assign ownership for patching and control debt
  • Exercise recovery dependencies in parallel

Days 61–90: automate safely

Automate bounded, measurable actions with clear human oversight.

  • Pilot one reversible self-healing pattern
  • Add decision logs and confidence thresholds
  • Measure avoided tickets and recovered capacity
  • Present an executive benefits and risk report
Recommended principle: governance first, controlled automation second. Automation should reduce known operational work, not conceal weak controls.
Evidence from regulated banking operations

Selected case studies

Organisation names and implementation details are anonymised. Results reflect delivered work, not toolkit projections.

75% less downtime

Resilience execution redesign

Recovery was constrained by sequential execution across interconnected services.

  • Parallelised recovery activities
  • Automated the recovery sequence
  • Reduced customer-facing recovery from four hours to one
34 incidents to 1

Patching governance redesign

Recurring patch-related incidents lacked end-to-end ownership and validation discipline.

  • Created a five-person governance squad
  • Introduced weekly review and green-zone controls
  • Scaled the model to additional business units
10% to 1%

Failed-change reduction

Failures repeatedly came from weak testing, off-window deployment and untested rollback plans.

  • Redesigned approval evidence
  • Closed the three repeat causal categories
  • Reduced the failed-change rate over three years
$156K annual saving

Alert noise optimisation

Static thresholds and poor suppression discipline generated avoidable handling cost.

  • Audited rules against real incidents
  • Embedded suppression into runbooks
  • Reduced weekly events from about 3,500 to 1,500
160 hours weekly

Controlled self-healing

Known recovery actions were being performed manually after ticket creation.

  • Moved auto-restart ahead of ticket creation
  • Reduced weekly incidents from 1,000 to 450
  • Recovered approximately $320K in annual capacity
44 to 9 incidents

Third-party resilience correction

A systemic vendor dependency created recurring major incidents.

  • Identified the architectural single point of failure
  • Escalated directly to vendor leadership
  • Created a recovery runbook and drove an architecture change
